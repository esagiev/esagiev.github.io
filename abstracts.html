Farsighted Cheap Talk on Networks (Abstract)

I developed a model of costless aggregation of costly information for competing farsighted agents. It required development of a comprehensive cheap talk model for multi-agent and multi-signal setup with costly signal acquisition, enriched by endogenous audience partition, retransmission of signals, and noisy signals partition. In particular, I introduced concepts of message falsity and alternation probability of sequence of messages, as I solved the bias-variance trade-off for receivers. Unlike other cheap talk models messages can be rejected by agents, if it upsets their balance between noisiness and informedness. As a result, the model revealed dominance of public over private messages, while audience composition is driven by balance of benefits between sender and her receivers. It also rules out repeated communication. Therefore, signal acquisition cost and audience partition are main factors of dynamic network formation. Since communication is dynamic and only noise matters, the solution is a set of reachability graphs.

Bayesian Persuasion in Tax Competition (Abstract)

Multinational enterprises form long supply chains through subsidiaries in large number of countries distributing profit between them. This practice may be motivated by secret tax preferences provided by some of them. Since no government has a complete view of the global business activity, truthful communication becomes a key issue in counteraction to harmful tax competition. To enquire into the problem I modeled information exchange between governments as a sequential Bayesian persuasion. This approach demonstrates how communication through long-chain structure can facilitate tax avoidance instead of preventing it; why tax rate levels are not a distinct feature of the countries supporting aggressive tax planning; how countries take their roles in profit shifting schemes; and I provide reasoning for tax planning schemes that exist in practice. The approach reveals that agreements on minimum tax rate and restricted information exchange have low efficiency in tackling tax avoidance. However, provision of technical support to national tax authorities might be a valid tool to alleviate the problem.

Bayesian Persuasion Mediating Cheap Talk (Abstract)

We study a multi-player model of sequential communication. A receiver obtains information from two sources. The first is cheap talk with a sender, who acquires the information, while the second is Bayesian persuasion with a mediator, who modifies the initial information. We prove that Bayesian persuasion does not defy truthful communication in cheap talk, but reduces the ex-ante utility of the sender. However, the fineness of the state-space partition in cheap talk can be altered to mitigate utility losses. We also demonstrate that a signalling rule in the persuasion is defined by whether the revealed partition element triggers an action of the receiver that is unfavourable to the mediator. In addition, we characterise preferences of the mediator regarding a cheap-talk partition formed by the sender. Finally, we illustrate our results drawing on a communication process between tax authorities.
